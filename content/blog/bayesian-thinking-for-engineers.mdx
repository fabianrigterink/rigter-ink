## Bayes' theorem in 30 seconds

At its core, Bayes' theorem updates beliefs given new evidence:

$$
P(H \mid D) = \frac{P(D \mid H) \cdot P(H)}{P(D)}
$$

- $P(H)$: your **prior** belief before seeing data
- $P(D \mid H)$: the **likelihood** of the data given the hypothesis
- $P(H \mid D)$: the **posterior** — your updated belief

## A/B testing: the Bayesian way

Classical A/B tests ask "is there a statistically significant difference?" Bayesian A/B tests ask a more useful question: "what's the probability that variant B is better than variant A, and by how much?"

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

def bayesian_ab_test(
    visitors_a: int, conversions_a: int,
    visitors_b: int, conversions_b: int,
    n_samples: int = 100_000,
) -> dict:
    """Run a Bayesian A/B test using Beta-Binomial conjugacy."""
    # Posterior distributions (Beta with uniform prior)
    alpha_a = conversions_a + 1
    beta_a = visitors_a - conversions_a + 1
    alpha_b = conversions_b + 1
    beta_b = visitors_b - conversions_b + 1

    samples_a = np.random.beta(alpha_a, beta_a, n_samples)
    samples_b = np.random.beta(alpha_b, beta_b, n_samples)

    prob_b_better = (samples_b > samples_a).mean()
    expected_lift = ((samples_b - samples_a) / samples_a).mean()

    return {
        "prob_b_better": prob_b_better,
        "expected_lift": expected_lift,
        "mean_a": samples_a.mean(),
        "mean_b": samples_b.mean(),
    }

result = bayesian_ab_test(
    visitors_a=10_000, conversions_a=480,
    visitors_b=10_000, conversions_b=520,
)
print(f"P(B > A) = {result['prob_b_better']:.1%}")
print(f"Expected lift: {result['expected_lift']:.2%}")
```

## Reasoning about incidents

When a production incident occurs, you're doing Bayesian reasoning whether you realize it or not. "The database is slow" — what's the probability it's a query regression vs. hardware failure vs. increased traffic?

Each piece of evidence (metrics, logs, recent deploys) updates your posterior. The engineers who debug fastest are those who maintain good priors (knowing common failure modes) and efficiently seek high-information evidence (checking the most discriminating signals first).

## Prior elicitation

The prior is often the most controversial part of Bayesian analysis. But having to make your assumptions explicit is a feature, not a bug. When you choose a prior, you're saying "here's what I believed before seeing the data." That transparency makes it easier to identify when assumptions are wrong.

For engineering applications, weakly informative priors work well. They encode basic domain knowledge (like "conversion rates are between 0% and 100%") without being overly opinionated.

$$
\text{Rate} \sim \text{Beta}(1, 1) \quad \text{(uniform — no prior knowledge)}
$$

$$
\text{Rate} \sim \text{Beta}(2, 20) \quad \text{(weakly informative — expect low rates)}
$$

The beauty of the Bayesian approach is that as you collect more data, the prior matters less and less. The data speaks for itself.
